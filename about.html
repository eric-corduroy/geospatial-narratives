Geospatial Narratives

Geospatial Narratives is a project and critical toolkit developed in response to the increasing visibility and narrative use of satellite imagery in today's media environment. At a time when deepfakes, disinformation and visual manipulation are becoming increasingly common, the ability to critically assess images – especially those that appear objective or “scientific” – is no longer a luxury, but a public necessity.

The project explores how satellite data is used to shape public narratives in journalism, crisis reporting, activism, and more. Through archiving and ( extended) contextualisation of these uses, Geospatial Narratives makes them searchable, comparable and accessible for critical reflection.

Once reserved exclusively for military or government agencies, satellite images are now omnipresent. But despite their ubiquity and increasing availability, they are often misunderstood, misinterpreted or taken for granted. Satellite images are commonly perceived as neutral, purely observational or even omniscient – particularly due to our conditioning by tools such as Google Earth, which make aerial imagery seem familiar and trustworthy. However, this visual familiarity is misleading.

Satellite images are not ‘images’ in the traditional photographic sense. They are data – scientifically measured, influenced by environmental factors and digitally processed. They require complex decisions about sensors, wavelengths, band combinations, image stitching and enhancement techniques, and are subject to control by companies and geopolitical factors in terms of access. Therefore, their use in the media – whether to document conflicts, visualise climate change, or simply embellish reporting – requires a more informed and critical literacy than is currently widespread.

Scholars from fields such as media studies and architecture, including Laura Kurgan and Lisa Parks, have long argued that the public should have access not only to images, but also to the context, technologies, and political frameworks that shape those images. As Kurgan already stated in 2013: ‘With every image, we should be able to ask questions about its technology, its location data, its ownership, its legibility, and its source.’

Geospatial Narratives responds to this need and attempts to create a framework for knowledge transfer in this regard.

The information presented on this platform combines explicit details from published articles with interpretive insights drawn from experience working with satellite imagery as both a visual and technical medium.

Some facts, such as the publication date, source, headline and geographical area referred to, are clear and taken directly from the articles. However, the satellite images accompanying these narratives often lack the necessary context. Who took the image? Which sensor was used? What exactly are we seeing?

To help address these gaps, I draw on publicly available data about satellite capabilities, visual clues within the imagery, and an interpretive sensibility developed through regular engagement with geospatial data. When the satellite or sensor is named, it's often possible to infer the type — optical, radar, or hyperspectral — based on known specifications. How the imagery is visualized (e.g. true color, false color, or derived indices like NDVI) depends on sensor characteristics, processing decisions, and the thematic focus of the article—be it flooding, wildfires, or deforestation.

Resolution , however, remains particularly difficult to determine. It's rarely stated, and often visually distorted by cropping, rescaling, or digital post-processing—making precise identification impossible. In such cases, I refer to the known native resolution of likely sensors and categorize images into three general resolution bands: <1m, 1–10m, or >10m.

Some degree of approximation and deviation is therefore unavoidable with this approach. But this interpretive margin is not a flaw it is part of the critical inquiry: How much do we really know when we are shown a satellite image? And what remains absent and unseen?

Curation for this platform follows an editorial practice: iterative, subjective, and open to new perspectives.

This applies not only to the content in the Index section, but also to the accompanying theoretical materials within Literacy. Both sections are designed as curated accompanying materials and conceptual tools to encourage a reflective engagement with the narrative and analytical use of satellite data. They do not claim to be exhaustive or academically authoritative, but rather invite open, interdisciplinary dialogue.

The index collects examples in which satellite images are used in journalistic, investigative, activist or artistic contexts. It traces how such images circulate and attempts to highlight their narrative, political and media dimensions - and at the same time to fill gaps in public documentation. The archive does not offer a comprehensive overview, but represents a curated selection characterised by narrative relevance, linguistic accessibility and a focus on predominantly Western, English-language sources. I recognise the limitations of this perspective and welcome suggestions for expansion.

The article list brings together a deliberately diverse range of perspectives: from (popular) scientific publications and media-theoretical or philosophical texts to journalistic reports and NGO-based analyses. It includes books, online articles, essays, interviews, and videos that exemplify how satellite imagery is used within cultural, political, and media discourse. The selection is guided by criteria such as narrative and analytical relevance, linguistic accessibility, and interdisciplinary significance.

The glossary expands this collection with a hybrid vocabulary: a mixture of technical terms, scientific concepts and critical or poetic figures of thought from fields such as remote sensing, media theory, design research and philosophy. Some entries provide basic definitions of how satellite technologies work, while others serve as food for thought – metaphors, conceptual frameworks or tools for questioning our perception and use of these technologies.

All entries (see Method) have been included without prior authorisation from the original authors or publications. Deletions may be requested at any time.

This platform serves as an open, collective, ever-evolving resource for the critical study of satellite data as media in narrative and investigative contexts. It invites contributions that expand the scope of the archive and promote a better public understanding of how satellite data is used, interpreted and circulated.

Submissions may fall into two categories:

Narrative Use: examples in which satellite imagery plays a central role in narratives, analyses or reports. These can be news articles, visual studies or artistic works that use satellite data for more than just illustration.

Satellite Literacy: contributions that promote a critical examination of the medium itself. This includes basic explanations as well as more interpretative or speculative considerations. Contributions may cover key concepts from the fields of remote sensing, media theory, design research, critical geography or philosophy.

Contributions from different application contexts, regions and cultures are welcome. This archive is based on the conviction that satellite images are never neutral. Their narrative potential, biases and blind spots only become apparent through different perspectives. Accordingly, I also recognize the limitations of my own perspective, which is primarily shaped by Western, English- and German-language media.

Submissions are reviewed based on their relevance, contextual clarity, and alignment with the platform’s editorial focus. Items used decoratively or without critical framing may fall outside the scope of the archive.
